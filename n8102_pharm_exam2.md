# N8102 Pharmacology — Antihypertensives Practice Exam

## Antihypertensive Pharmacology

1. A 68-year-old female with a history of hypertension and osteoporosis is currently uncontrolled on amlodipine monotherapy. The provider decides to add a second first-line antihypertensive agent. Which medication class would provide a beneficial secondary effect for her osteoporosis by promoting calcium retention?

   [( )] Loop diuretics
   [(X)] Thiazide diuretics
   [( )] Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs)
   [( )] Beta-blockers

   ****Rationale:****
   Thiazide diuretics decrease calcium excretion, leading to mild hypercalcemia/calcium retention, which reduces osteoporosis risk in at-risk patients. Loop diuretics cause calcium wasting and would worsen bone density. ACEIs and beta-blockers have no direct effect on calcium retention or bone density.

2. A patient with Stage 1 hypertension and a PREVENT score of 9% is started on lisinopril 10 mg daily. According to the 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines, when should this patient be scheduled to return to the clinic to reassess blood pressure, medication adherence, and tolerability?

   [( )] 1 week
   [(X)] 1 month
   [( )] 3 months
   [( )] 6 months

   ****Rationale:****
   The 2025 ACC/AHA algorithm calls for reassessment 1 month after initiating or adjusting a first-line agent. One week is too soon to see the drug's maximal effect (2-4 weeks), while 3-6 months is the reassessment window for patients managed with lifestyle modification alone.

3. Beta-blockers lower blood pressure primarily by reducing heart rate and cardiac output (negative chronotropy and inotropy). However, they possess an additional mechanism in the kidneys that contributes to their antihypertensive effect. What is this secondary mechanism?

   [( )] Direct blockade of the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC)
   [(X)] Inhibition of renin release from the juxtaglomerular cells
   [( )] Stimulation of bradykinin production
   [( )] Dilation of the afferent arteriole

   ****Rationale:****
   Beta-1 receptors on the juxtaglomerular cells trigger renin release; beta-blockers inhibit this, suppressing RAAS activation and lowering systemic vascular resistance over time. ENaC blockade describes potassium-sparing diuretics, bradykinin stimulation describes ACE inhibitors, and beta-blockers do not directly dilate the afferent arteriole.

4. A patient with bipolar disorder is well-controlled on lithium. They are newly diagnosed with hypertension, and the provider is considering starting an ACE inhibitor. What is the most significant risk of this specific drug combination?

   [( )] The ACE inhibitor will decrease lithium concentrations, leading to a manic episode.
   [( )] The combination will cause a dangerous prolongation of the QT interval.
   [(X)] The ACE inhibitor will increase lithium concentrations, potentially leading to lithium toxicity.
   [( )] Lithium will completely block the antihypertensive effect of the ACE inhibitor.

   ****Rationale:****
   ACE inhibitors increase lithium concentrations, which can precipitate toxicity given lithium's narrow therapeutic index. The direction is an increase, not a decrease, in lithium levels; QT prolongation is not a classic feature of this interaction; and lithium does not block the ACE inhibitor's antihypertensive effect (NSAIDs do that).

5. A patient with advanced chronic kidney disease (CKD) requires a loop diuretic for volume overload. The provider prescribes a significantly higher dose of intravenous furosemide than would be used for a patient with normal renal function. What is the physiological rationale for this higher dose?

   [( )] Loop diuretics are metabolized heavily by the liver in patients with CKD.
   [(X)] The kidneys are the site of action; reduced renal blood flow requires higher doses to deliver the drug to the tubular lumen.
   [( )] CKD causes an upregulation of beta-1 receptors that must be overcome.
   [( )] Patients with CKD have an increased volume of distribution that dilutes the drug.

   ****Rationale:****
   Loop diuretics act from inside the tubular lumen after being actively secreted there; reduced renal blood flow in CKD means higher doses are needed to reach an effective concentration at the site of action. They are cleared renally rather than hepatically, CKD doesn't upregulate beta-1 receptors, and volume of distribution changes aren't the driving factor here.

6. Which of the following over-the-counter medications or substances are known to elevate blood pressure and reduce the efficacy of antihypertensive therapy? (Select all that apply)

   [[X]] Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
   [[X]] Pseudoephedrine (Decongestants)
   [[ ]] Acetaminophen
   [[X]] Licorice root supplements
   [[ ]] Saline nasal spray

   ****Rationale:****
   NSAIDs cause fluid retention and blunt antihypertensive efficacy, sympathomimetics like pseudoephedrine raise blood pressure directly, and licorice root has mineralocorticoid activity that raises blood pressure. Acetaminophen is the recommended safer alternative to NSAIDs for pain, and saline nasal spray is the recommended safer alternative to systemic decongestants.

7. A patient brings a new prescription bottle to the pharmacy. The generic name of the medication ends in the suffix "-dipine" (e.g., felodipine, isradipine). Based on this suffix, what is the primary mechanism of action of this drug?

   [( )] It blocks the AT1 receptor.
   [( )] It inhibits the Na+/Cl- cotransporter in the distal convoluted tubule.
   [(X)] It blocks L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle, causing vasodilation.
   [( )] It acts as a central alpha-2 receptor agonist.

   ****Rationale:****
   The "-dipine" suffix marks dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, which block L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle to cause vasodilation. AT1 blockade describes ARBs ("-sartan"), Na+/Cl- cotransporter inhibition describes thiazides, and central alpha-2 agonism describes clonidine/methyldopa.

8. A patient is being evaluated for resistant hypertension. The provider notes that the patient is currently taking doxazosin 8 mg daily. While doxazosin can cause a "first-dose phenomenon," what is a major adverse effect associated with its *chronic* administration that often leads to worsening hypertension over time?

   [(X)] Water and sodium accumulation (edema)
   [( )] Hypertrophy of the left ventricle
   [( )] Severe hyperkalemia
   [( )] Chronic dry cough

   ****Rationale:****
   Chronic alpha-1 blocker use causes water and sodium accumulation, leading to edema, weight gain, and worsening hypertension over time — a key reason these agents are last-line. LVH, severe hyperkalemia, and dry cough are not classic adverse effects of this drug class.

9. A 55-year-old male with hypertension and newly diagnosed diabetic nephropathy with proteinuria is seen in the clinic. Which class of antihypertensive medications is strongly preferred for this patient because it reduces proteinuria and slows the progression of kidney disease?

   [( )] Alpha-1 receptor antagonists
   [( )] Beta-blockers
   [(X)] Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs)
   [( )] Potassium-sparing diuretics

   ****Rationale:****
   ACE inhibitors (and ARBs) reduce proteinuria and slow CKD progression in diabetic nephropathy specifically. The other classes may lower blood pressure but don't offer this targeted renal protective benefit.

10. A patient with severe, refractory hypertension is prescribed hydralazine. The pharmacist counsels the patient on the importance of adherence. What characteristic of hydralazine dosing makes long-term patient adherence particularly difficult?

    [( )] It must be taken on an empty stomach at 3:00 AM.
    [(X)] It requires three to four times daily (TID/QID) dosing.
    [( )] It is only available as a subcutaneous injection.
    [( )] It must be dissolved in a full glass of potassium-rich juice.

    ****Rationale:****
    Hydralazine's short half-life requires TID/QID dosing, which is a well-documented barrier to adherence and part of why it's reserved as a last-line agent. The other options describe fabricated constraints not applicable to hydralazine.

11. Some beta-blockers, such as acebutolol and pindolol, possess Intrinsic Sympathomimetic Activity (ISA). What does this mean pharmacologically?

    [( )] They cross the blood-brain barrier more rapidly than other agents.
    [(X)] They act as partial agonists, causing activation of adrenergic receptors while blocking endogenous catecholamines.
    [( )] They selectively block alpha-1 receptors in addition to beta receptors.
    [( )] They directly stimulate the release of nitric oxide from the endothelium.

    ****Rationale:****
    ISA means the drug is a partial agonist — it mildly activates adrenergic receptors while blocking the more potent endogenous catecholamines, a property that hasn't translated into strong clinical blood pressure benefit. Lipophilicity (not ISA) determines blood-brain barrier crossing, combined alpha/beta blockade describes carvedilol/labetalol (which lack ISA), and direct nitric oxide release is a property of nebivolol.

12. A patient with a history of severe, life-threatening asthma requires an antihypertensive medication. Which of the following drugs would be the SAFEST choice to avoid triggering a bronchospasm?

    [( )] Propranolol
    [( )] Carvedilol
    [( )] Labetalol
    [(X)] Amlodipine

    ****Rationale:****
    Amlodipine, a dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker, has no effect on beta receptors in the lungs and carries no bronchospasm risk. Propranolol, carvedilol, and labetalol all have non-selective beta-blockade that can trigger bronchospasm in severe asthmatics.

13. A patient with a history of severe hypertension presents to the clinic. The provider confirms the diagnosis and decides to initiate pharmacotherapy. According to the 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines, what is the primary *diastolic* blood pressure target for adults with confirmed hypertension?

    [( )] < 90 mm Hg
    [( )] < 85 mm Hg
    [(X)] < 80 mm Hg
    [( )] < 70 mm Hg

    ****Rationale:****
    The 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines set a Class 1 diastolic blood pressure target of < 80 mm Hg for adults with confirmed hypertension at increased risk.

14. A patient in the oncology ward develops severe hypercalcemia of malignancy. The medical team decides to utilize a diuretic to help promote the renal excretion of calcium. Which medication is most appropriate for this indication?

    [( )] Hydrochlorothiazide
    [(X)] Furosemide
    [( )] Spironolactone
    [( )] Chlorthalidone

    ****Rationale:****
    Loop diuretics like furosemide cause calcium wasting (hypocalcemia), making them useful for treating hypercalcemia of malignancy. Thiazides (hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone) cause calcium retention and would worsen the condition, and spironolactone doesn't have a significant calcium-wasting effect.

15. A 42-year-old patient with Stage 2 hypertension is prescribed a fixed-dose combination pill containing lisinopril and hydrochlorothiazide (Zestoretic). According to the lecture, what is the primary clinical advantage of using a fixed-dose combination pill rather than prescribing the two drugs separately?

    [( )] It prevents the development of drug-induced lupus.
    [(X)] It improves long-term patient adherence to the medication regimen.
    [( )] It completely eliminates the risk of hyperkalemia.
    [( )] It allows the medications to bypass hepatic first-pass metabolism.

    ****Rationale:****
    Taking one combination pill instead of two separate pills meaningfully improves adherence, one of the biggest barriers to long-term blood pressure control. Fixed-dose combinations don't prevent drug-induced lupus, don't eliminate hyperkalemia risk, and don't affect first-pass metabolism.

16. A patient is prescribed clonidine. While it is not a first-line antihypertensive, the provider notes it has several off-label and secondary uses. Which of the following conditions can clonidine be used to manage due to its ability to decrease sympathetic outflow? (Select all that apply)

    [[X]] Opioid withdrawal
    [[X]] Alcohol withdrawal
    [[X]] Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
    [[ ]] Hypercalcemia
    [[ ]] Asthma exacerbation

    ****Rationale:****
    Clonidine's central alpha-2 agonism suppresses sympathetic outflow, which is useful for managing the sympathetic surge in opioid and alcohol withdrawal, as well as for ADHD. It has no role in hypercalcemia or asthma, since it doesn't bronchodilate.

17. A patient taking chronic, high-dose furosemide for heart failure begins to experience diminished diuretic effects over time (diuretic tolerance). What structural adaptation in the nephron is primarily responsible for this specific type of tolerance?

    [( )] Atrophy of the proximal tubule
    [(X)] Hypertrophy of the distal tubules and collecting ducts
    [( )] Destruction of the glomerulus
    [( )] Dilation of the afferent arteriole

    ****Rationale:****
    Chronic loop diuretic use dumps excess sodium into the distal tubule, which compensates by hypertrophying and increasing its own sodium reabsorption — this is the structural basis of diuretic tolerance. The other listed changes don't occur as a compensatory response to loop diuretics.

18. A 35-year-old female is diagnosed with hypertension. The provider is considering starting an ACE inhibitor but notes a specific contraindication in her medical history. Which of the following conditions is an absolute contraindication to the use of an ACE inhibitor?

    [(X)] Bilateral renal artery stenosis
    [( )] History of gout
    [( )] Osteoporosis
    [( )] Migraine headaches

    ****Rationale:****
    In bilateral renal artery stenosis, the kidneys depend on angiotensin II-mediated efferent arteriole constriction to maintain glomerular filtration pressure; blocking this with an ACE inhibitor can precipitate acute renal failure. The other conditions are not contraindications to ACE inhibitor use.

19. A patient is prescribed a hydrophilic beta-blocker, such as atenolol or nadolol. Because of its low lipid solubility, the clearance of this medication is highly dependent on which of the following?

    [( )] Hepatic CYP450 enzymes
    [( )] Biliary excretion
    [(X)] Renal function
    [( )] Pulmonary exhalation

    ****Rationale:****
    Hydrophilic beta-blockers don't cross membranes as readily and aren't extensively metabolized by the liver, so they depend heavily on renal clearance. Lipophilic agents like propranolol rely on hepatic metabolism and biliary excretion instead, and pulmonary exhalation isn't a primary clearance route for these drugs.

20. Triamterene and amiloride are potassium-sparing diuretics. Unlike spironolactone, they do not block the mineralocorticoid (aldosterone) receptor. What is their specific mechanism of action?

    [( )] They inhibit the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in the Loop of Henle.
    [(X)] They block epithelial sodium channels (ENaC) in the collecting duct.
    [( )] They inhibit carbonic anhydrase in the proximal tubule.
    [( )] They directly dilate the efferent arteriole.

    ****Rationale:****
    Triamterene and amiloride block ENaC directly in the collecting duct, preventing sodium reabsorption and the coupled potassium excretion that goes with it. Na-K-2Cl cotransporter inhibition describes loop diuretics, and carbonic anhydrase inhibition describes acetazolamide.

21. A patient brings a new prescription bottle to the pharmacy. The generic name of the medication ends in the suffix "-pril" (e.g., benazepril, ramipril). Based on this suffix, what adverse effect should the pharmacist counsel the patient to monitor for?

    [( )] Hirsutism
    [(X)] Persistent, dry cough
    [( )] Gynecomastia
    [( )] Constipation

    ****Rationale:****
    The "-pril" suffix identifies ACE inhibitors, whose defining adverse effect is a persistent dry cough (up to 20% of patients) from bradykinin accumulation. Hirsutism is associated with minoxidil, gynecomastia with spironolactone, and constipation with verapamil.

22. A patient with angina pectoris is prescribed a calcium channel blocker (CCB). How do CCBs therapeutically relieve the symptoms of angina?

    [( )] By increasing myocardial oxygen demand
    [(X)] By causing coronary and peripheral vasodilation, which reduces myocardial oxygen demand
    [( )] By increasing the heart rate to improve coronary blood flow
    [( )] By acting as a direct diuretic to reduce blood volume

    ****Rationale:****
    CCBs dilate both coronary arteries (improving blood flow to the heart) and peripheral arteries (reducing afterload), which lowers overall cardiac workload and myocardial oxygen demand. Increasing oxygen demand or heart rate would worsen angina, and CCBs aren't diuretics.

23. Which of the following are recognized therapeutic uses for beta-blockers beyond the management of hypertension? (Select all that apply)

    [[X]] Migraine prophylaxis
    [[X]] Management of hyperthyroidism symptoms (e.g., tachycardia, tremor)
    [[X]] Glaucoma (as topical drops)
    [[ ]] Treatment of acute gout flares
    [[X]] Reduction of mortality post-myocardial infarction

    ****Rationale:****
    Beta-blockers have established roles in migraine prophylaxis, controlling hyperthyroid symptoms like tachycardia and tremor, lowering intraocular pressure as topical glaucoma drops, and reducing mortality after MI. They have no role in treating acute gout flares, which are managed with NSAIDs, colchicine, or steroids.

24. A patient with Stage 2 hypertension requires initiation of two first-line agents from different classes. Which of the following represents a rational, guideline-supported two-drug regimen?

    [( )] Lisinopril and Losartan
    [(X)] Amlodipine and Chlorthalidone
    [( )] Metoprolol and Clonidine
    [( )] Hydralazine and Minoxidil

    ****Rationale:****
    Amlodipine (a DHP calcium channel blocker) and chlorthalidone (a thiazide diuretic) are two first-line agents from different classes, matching guideline recommendations for Stage 2 hypertension. Lisinopril and losartan both act on the RAAS pathway and shouldn't be combined due to increased toxicity risk without added benefit; the other pairing uses last-line rather than first-line agents.

25. A patient is prescribed verapamil for hypertension and migraine prophylaxis. The patient later presents to the clinic with profound bradycardia. Why does verapamil carry a higher risk of bradycardia compared to amlodipine?

    [(X)] Verapamil is a non-DHP CCB that directly slows AV nodal conduction.
    [( )] Verapamil is a potent central alpha-2 agonist.
    [( )] Verapamil crosses the blood-brain barrier to inhibit the vagus nerve.
    [( )] Verapamil causes severe volume depletion, leading to a reflexive drop in heart rate.

    ****Rationale:****
    Verapamil is a non-dihydropyridine CCB that acts directly on cardiac tissue to slow AV nodal conduction, unlike DHP CCBs (like amlodipine) which act only on the vasculature. It's not an alpha-2 agonist, doesn't inhibit the vagus nerve via the CNS, and doesn't cause the volume depletion described.
