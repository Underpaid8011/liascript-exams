# N8102 Pharmacology — Antihypertensives Practice Exam

**1. A 68-year-old female with a history of hypertension and osteoporosis is currently uncontrolled on amlodipine monotherapy. The provider decides to add a second first-line antihypertensive agent. Which medication class would provide a beneficial secondary effect for her osteoporosis by promoting calcium retention?**
- [( )] A) Loop diuretics
- [(x)] B) Thiazide diuretics
- [( )] C) Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs)
- [( )] D) Beta-blockers
***
**Rationale:** Thiazide diuretics (unlike loop diuretics) decrease calcium excretion, leading to mild hypercalcemia/calcium retention. The lecture explicitly notes that in older patients at risk for osteoporosis, being on a thiazide diuretic for blood pressure actually reduces the risk of osteoporosis, making it a beneficial side effect.
* *Incorrect A:* Loop diuretics cause calcium wasting (hypocalcemia) and would worsen bone density.
* *Incorrect C, D:* ACEIs and Beta-blockers do not have a direct effect on calcium retention or bone density.
***

**2. A patient with Stage 1 hypertension and a PREVENT score of 9% is started on lisinopril 10 mg daily. According to the 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines, when should this patient be scheduled to return to the clinic to reassess blood pressure, medication adherence, and tolerability?**
- [( )] A) 1 week
- [(x)] B) 1 month
- [( )] C) 3 months
- [( )] D) 6 months
***
**Rationale:** The 2025 ACC/AHA hypertension treatment algorithm clearly states that after initiating a first-line agent (Step 1) or adding subsequent agents, the provider should "Reassess in 1 month" to evaluate blood pressure, adherence, and medication tolerance.
* *Incorrect A, C, D:* 1 week is too soon to see the maximal effect of the drug (which takes 2-4 weeks). 3 to 6 months is the reassessment timeframe for patients managed on *lifestyle modifications only*.
***

**3. Beta-blockers lower blood pressure primarily by reducing heart rate and cardiac output (negative chronotropy and inotropy). However, they possess an additional mechanism in the kidneys that contributes to their antihypertensive effect. What is this secondary mechanism?**
- [( )] A) Direct blockade of the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC)
- [(x)] B) Inhibition of renin release from the juxtaglomerular cells
- [( )] C) Stimulation of bradykinin production
- [( )] D) Dilation of the afferent arteriole
***
**Rationale:** Beta-1 receptors are located not only on the heart but also on the juxtaglomerular (JG) cells of the kidneys. Beta-blockers inhibit the release of renin from these cells, which suppresses the activation of the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS), contributing to decreased systemic vascular resistance over time.
* *Incorrect A:* ENaC blockade is the mechanism of potassium-sparing diuretics like amiloride.
* *Incorrect C:* Bradykinin stimulation is associated with ACE inhibitors.
* *Incorrect D:* Beta-blockers do not directly dilate the afferent arteriole.
***

**4. A patient with bipolar disorder is well-controlled on lithium. They are newly diagnosed with hypertension, and the provider is considering starting an ACE inhibitor. What is the most significant risk of this specific drug combination?**
- [( )] A) The ACE inhibitor will decrease lithium concentrations, leading to a manic episode.
- [( )] B) The combination will cause a dangerous prolongation of the QT interval.
- [(x)] C) The ACE inhibitor will increase lithium concentrations, potentially leading to lithium toxicity.
- [( )] D) Lithium will completely block the antihypertensive effect of the ACE inhibitor.
***
**Rationale:** The slide detailing ACE inhibitor drug interactions specifically lists Lithium, noting that ACE inhibitors cause "increased lithium concentrations." This can easily lead to lithium toxicity, which has a very narrow therapeutic index.
* *Incorrect A:* It increases, rather than decreases, lithium levels.
* *Incorrect B:* QT prolongation is not a classic interaction between ACEIs and lithium.
* *Incorrect D:* Lithium does not block the antihypertensive effect of ACEIs (NSAIDs do this).
***

**5. A patient with advanced chronic kidney disease (CKD) requires a loop diuretic for volume overload. The provider prescribes a significantly higher dose of intravenous furosemide than would be used for a patient with normal renal function. What is the physiological rationale for this higher dose?**
- [( )] A) Loop diuretics are metabolized heavily by the liver in patients with CKD.
- [(x)] B) The kidneys are the site of action; reduced renal blood flow requires higher doses to deliver the drug to the tubular lumen.
- [( )] C) CKD causes an upregulation of beta-1 receptors that must be overcome.
- [( )] D) Patients with CKD have an increased volume of distribution that dilutes the drug.
***
**Rationale:** Loop diuretics are actively secreted into the renal tubule, and their site of action is *inside* the kidney (the thick ascending limb). In chronic kidney disease, renal blood flow and drug delivery to the tubular lumen are reduced. Therefore, a paradoxical approach is required: higher doses must be given to "punch through" and reach the site of action.
* *Incorrect A:* They are cleared primarily by the kidneys, not heavily metabolized by the liver.
* *Incorrect C:* CKD does not upregulate beta-1 receptors.
* *Incorrect D:* Volume of distribution changes do not explain the primary need for massive loop doses in CKD; tubular delivery does.
***

- [[x]] B) Pseudoephedrine (Decongestants)
- [[ ]] C) Acetaminophen
- [[x]] D) Licorice root supplements
- [[ ]] E) Saline nasal spray

**Rationale:** The source material provides a specific list of substances that elevate blood pressure. This includes NSAIDs (which cause fluid retention and reduce antihypertensive efficacy), Sympathomimetics like pseudoephedrine (decongestants), and Herbal supplements like licorice root (which has mineralocorticoid activity).

**7. A patient brings a new prescription bottle to the pharmacy. The generic name of the medication ends in the suffix "-dipine" (e.g., felodipine, isradipine). Based on this suffix, what is the primary mechanism of action of this drug?**
- [( )] A) It blocks the AT1 receptor.
- [( )] B) It inhibits the Na+/Cl- cotransporter in the distal convoluted tubule.
- [(x)] C) It blocks L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle, causing vasodilation.
- [( )] D) It acts as a central alpha-2 receptor agonist.
***
**Rationale:** The suffix "-dipine" is the safe suffix for dihydropyridine (DHP) calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine). Their mechanism of action is blocking L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle, which prevents calcium entry and causes vasodilation.
* *Incorrect A:* Blocking the AT1 receptor is the mechanism of ARBs ("-sartan").
* *Incorrect B:* Inhibiting the Na+/Cl- cotransporter is the mechanism of thiazides.
* *Incorrect D:* Central alpha-2 agonism is the mechanism of clonidine/methyldopa.

**8. A patient is being evaluated for resistant hypertension. The provider notes that the patient is currently taking doxazosin 8 mg daily. While doxazosin can cause a "first-dose phenomenon," what is a major adverse effect associated with its *chronic* administration that often leads to worsening hypertension over time?**
- [(x)] A) Water and sodium accumulation (edema)
- [( )] B) Hypertrophy of the left ventricle
- [( )] C) Severe hyperkalemia
- [( )] D) Chronic dry cough
***
**Rationale:** Doxazosin is an alpha-1 receptor antagonist. The lecture explicitly states that with *chronic administration*, these drugs cause water and sodium accumulation. This leads to edema, weight gain, and worsening hypertension over time, which is why they are considered last-line agents.
* *Incorrect B, C, D:* LVH, severe hyperkalemia, and dry cough are not classic adverse effects of alpha-1 blockers.
***

**9. A 55-year-old male with hypertension and newly diagnosed diabetic nephropathy with proteinuria is seen in the clinic. Which class of antihypertensive medications is strongly preferred for this patient because it reduces proteinuria and slows the progression of kidney disease?**
- [( )] A) Alpha-1 receptor antagonists
- [( )] B) Beta-blockers
- [(x)] C) Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs)
- [( )] D) Potassium-sparing diuretics
***
**Rationale:** ACE inhibitors (and ARBs) are explicitly highlighted as great drugs for patients with diabetic nephropathy, especially when proteinuria is present. They reduce proteinuria and slow the progression of chronic kidney disease.
* *Incorrect A, B, D:* While these can lower blood pressure, they do not offer the specific, primary renal protective benefits against diabetic proteinuria that ACEIs/ARBs provide.

**10. A patient with severe, refractory hypertension is prescribed hydralazine. The pharmacist counsels the patient on the importance of adherence. What characteristic of hydralazine dosing makes long-term patient adherence particularly difficult?**

- [(x)] B) It requires three to four times daily (TID/QID) dosing.
- [( )] C) It is only available as a subcutaneous injection.
- [( )] D) It must be dissolved in a full glass of potassium-rich juice.
***
**Rationale:** Hydralazine has a short half-life and requires dosing three to four times daily (TID/QID). The lecturer explicitly notes that having to take a medication TID is "very bad for adherence," making it a last-line agent for common hypertension.
* *Incorrect A, C, D:* These are fabricated constraints not applicable to hydralazine.

**11. Some beta-blockers, such as acebutolol and pindolol, possess Intrinsic Sympathomimetic Activity (ISA). What does this mean pharmacologically?**
- [( )] A) They cross the blood-brain barrier more rapidly than other agents.
- [(x)] B) They act as partial agonists, causing activation of adrenergic receptors while blocking endogenous catecholamines.
- [( )] C) They selectively block alpha-1 receptors in addition to beta receptors.
- [( )] D) They directly stimulate the release of nitric oxide from the endothelium.
***
**Rationale:** Intrinsic Sympathomimetic Activity (ISA) means the drug acts as a partial agonist. It causes mild activation of the adrenergic receptors while simultaneously blocking the more potent endogenous catecholamines. The lecturer notes this concept "didn't work" well clinically for blood pressure, and these drugs are rarely used.
* *Incorrect A:* Lipid solubility (lipophilicity) dictates BBB crossing, not ISA.
* *Incorrect C:* Combined alpha/beta blockade is seen with carvedilol/labetalol, which do not have ISA.
* *Incorrect D:* Direct nitric oxide release is a property of nebivolol, not the definition of ISA.

**12. A patient with a history of severe, life-threatening asthma requires an antihypertensive medication. Which of the following drugs would be the SAFEST choice to avoid triggering a bronchospasm?**
- [( )] A) Propranolol
- [( )] B) Carvedilol
- [( )] C) Labetalol
- [(x)] D) Amlodipine
***
**Rationale:** Amlodipine is a DHP Calcium Channel Blocker. It has no effect on beta receptors in the lungs and carries zero risk of inducing bronchospasm. (Note: While cardioselective beta-blockers are *safer* than non-selective ones, in a patient with *severe, life-threatening* asthma, a CCB is entirely free of this risk).
* *Incorrect A, B, C:* Propranolol, Carvedilol, and Labetalol are non-selective beta-blockers. They block beta-2 receptors in the lungs and carry a high risk of triggering bronchospasm in asthmatics.
***

**13. A patient with a history of severe hypertension presents to the clinic. The provider confirms the diagnosis and decides to initiate pharmacotherapy. According to the 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines, what is the primary *diastolic* blood pressure target for adults with confirmed hypertension?**
- [( )] A) < 90 mm Hg
- [( )] B) < 85 mm Hg
- [(x)] C) < 80 mm Hg
- [( )] D) < 70 mm Hg
***
**Rationale:** The 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines state that for adults with confirmed hypertension who are at increased risk, the primary diastolic blood pressure (DBP) target is < 80 mm Hg (Class 1 recommendation).
* *Incorrect A, B, D:* The goal is explicitly < 80 mm Hg.

**14. A patient in the oncology ward develops severe hypercalcemia of malignancy. The medical team decides to utilize a diuretic to help promote the renal excretion of calcium. Which medication is most appropriate for this indication?**
- [( )] A) Hydrochlorothiazide
- [(x)] B) Furosemide
- [( )] C) Spironolactone
- [( )] D) Chlorthalidone
***
**Rationale:** Loop diuretics (like furosemide) cause marked natriuresis with the loss of several electrolytes, specifically including calcium (hypocalcemia). Because they cause calcium wasting, they are utilized therapeutically to manage hypercalcemia of malignancy.
* *Incorrect A, D:* Thiazides (HCTZ, chlorthalidone) cause calcium *retention* (hypercalcemia) and would worsen the patient's condition.
* *Incorrect C:* Spironolactone is potassium-sparing and does not have a profound calcium-wasting effect.

**15. A 42-year-old patient with Stage 2 hypertension is prescribed a fixed-dose combination pill containing lisinopril and hydrochlorothiazide (Zestoretic). According to the lecture, what is the primary clinical advantage of using a fixed-dose combination pill rather than prescribing the two drugs separately?**
- [( )] A) It prevents the development of drug-induced lupus.
- [(x)] B) It improves long-term patient adherence to the medication regimen.
- [( )] C) It completely eliminates the risk of hyperkalemia.
- [( )] D) It allows the medications to bypass hepatic first-pass metabolism.
***
**Rationale:** The guidelines are "very emphatic" about using combination pills (fixed-dose combinations). The primary clinical advantage is that taking one pill instead of two vastly improves patient compliance/adherence, which is one of the biggest barriers to long-term blood pressure control.
* *Incorrect A, C, D:* Fixed-dose combinations do not prevent DILE, eliminate hyperkalemia (though a thiazide might balance an ACEI's potassium effect, it doesn't *eliminate* the risk), or bypass first-pass metabolism.
***

**16. A patient is prescribed clonidine. While it is not a first-line antihypertensive, the provider notes it has several off-label and secondary uses. Which of the following conditions can clonidine be used to manage due to its ability to decrease sympathetic outflow? (Select all that apply)**
- [[x]] A) Opioid withdrawal
- [[x]] B) Alcohol withdrawal
- [[x]] C) Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
- [[ ]] D) Hypercalcemia
- [[ ]] E) Asthma exacerbation
***
**Rationale:** Clonidine is a central alpha-2 agonist that heavily suppresses sympathetic nervous system outflow. Because of this, it is used off-label to mitigate the sympathetic "storm" (agitation, tachycardia) seen in opioid withdrawal and alcohol withdrawal, and is also used for ADHD.
* *Incorrect D, E:* It has no role in managing hypercalcemia or asthma (it does not bronchodilate).
***

**17. A patient taking chronic, high-dose furosemide for heart failure begins to experience diminished diuretic effects over time (diuretic tolerance). What structural adaptation in the nephron is primarily responsible for this specific type of tolerance?**
- [( )] A) Atrophy of the proximal tubule
- [(x)] B) Hypertrophy of the distal tubules and collecting ducts
- [( )] C) Destruction of the glomerulus
- [( )] D) Dilation of the afferent arteriole
***
**Rationale:** Chronic use of loop diuretics blocks sodium reabsorption in the Loop of Henle, dumping massive amounts of sodium into the distal tubule. The distal tubule adapts to this heavy sodium load by undergoing hypertrophy (enlargement) and increasing its own sodium reabsorption, leading to diuretic tolerance/resistance.
* *Incorrect A, C, D:* These structural changes do not occur as a compensatory mechanism to loop diuretics.
***

**18. A 35-year-old female is diagnosed with hypertension. The provider is considering starting an ACE inhibitor but notes a specific contraindication in her medical history. Which of the following conditions is an absolute contraindication to the use of an ACE inhibitor?**
- [(x)] A) Bilateral renal artery stenosis
- [( )] B) History of gout
- [( )] C) Osteoporosis
- [( )] D) Migraine headaches
***
**Rationale:** Bilateral renal artery stenosis is a strict contraindication to the use of ACE inhibitors (and ARBs). In this condition, the kidneys rely on Angiotensin II-mediated efferent arteriole constriction to maintain glomerular filtration pressure; blocking this with an ACEI can precipitate acute renal failure.
* *Incorrect B, C, D:* These are not contraindications for ACE inhibitors.
***

**19. A patient is prescribed a hydrophilic beta-blocker, such as atenolol or nadolol. Because of its low lipid solubility, the clearance of this medication is highly dependent on which of the following?**
- [( )] A) Hepatic CYP450 enzymes
- [( )] B) Biliary excretion
- [(x)] C) Renal function
- [( )] D) Pulmonary exhalation
***
**Rationale:** Hydrophilic (water-soluble) beta-blockers, such as atenolol and nadolol, do not readily cross cell membranes or the blood-brain barrier. Because they are not easily metabolized by the liver, they depend heavily on renal function for elimination.
* *Incorrect A, B:* Lipophilic drugs (like propranolol) rely on hepatic metabolism and biliary excretion.
* *Incorrect D:* Drugs are not primarily cleared via pulmonary exhalation.
***

**20. Triamterene and amiloride are potassium-sparing diuretics. Unlike spironolactone, they do not block the mineralocorticoid (aldosterone) receptor. What is their specific mechanism of action?**
- [( )] A) They inhibit the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in the Loop of Henle.
- [(x)] B) They block epithelial sodium channels (ENaC) in the collecting duct.
- [( )] C) They inhibit carbonic anhydrase in the proximal tubule.
- [( )] D) They directly dilate the efferent arteriole.
***
**Rationale:** Triamterene and amiloride work in the collecting duct by directly blocking the epithelial sodium channels (ENaC). This prevents sodium reabsorption, which in turn prevents the coupled excretion of potassium, making them potassium-sparing.
* *Incorrect A:* This is the mechanism of loop diuretics.
* *Incorrect C:* This is the mechanism of acetazolamide.
* *Incorrect D:* This is not a primary mechanism of diuretics.
***

**22. A patient with angina pectoris is prescribed a calcium channel blocker (CCB). How do CCBs therapeutically relieve the symptoms of angina?**
- [( )] A) By increasing myocardial oxygen demand
- [(x)] B) By causing coronary and peripheral vasodilation, which reduces myocardial oxygen demand
- [( )] C) By increasing the heart rate to improve coronary blood flow
- [( )] D) By acting as a direct diuretic to reduce blood volume
***
**Rationale:** Calcium channel blockers (CCBs) relieve angina pectoris by causing vasodilation of both the coronary arteries (improving blood flow to the heart muscle) and peripheral arteries (decreasing afterload/SVR). This reduces the overall workload of the heart and decreases myocardial oxygen demand.
* *Incorrect A, C:* Increasing oxygen demand or heart rate would *worsen* angina.
* *Incorrect D:* CCBs are not diuretics.

**24. A patient with Stage 2 hypertension requires initiation of two first-line agents from different classes. Which of the following represents a rational, guideline-supported two-drug regimen?**
- [( )] A) Lisinopril and Losartan
- [(x)] B) Amlodipine and Chlorthalidone
- [( )] C) Metoprolol and Clonidine
- [( )] D) Hydralazine and Minoxidil
***
**Rationale:** For Stage 2 hypertension, the guidelines recommend starting *two* first-line agents from *different* classes. Amlodipine is a DHP CCB and Chlorthalidone is a thiazide diuretic. This is a rational, guideline-supported combination.
* *Incorrect A:* Lisinopril (ACEI) and Losartan (ARB) work on the exact same RAAS pathway. Combining them increases toxicity (hyperkalemia, AKI) without added benefit and is contraindicated.
* *Incorrect C, D:* Clonidine, Hydralazine, and Minoxidil are last-line agents, not first-line agents.

**25. A patient is prescribed verapamil for hypertension and migraine prophylaxis. The patient later presents to the clinic with profound bradycardia. Why does verapamil carry a higher risk of bradycardia compared to amlodipine?**
- [(x)] A) Verapamil is a non-DHP CCB that directly slows AV nodal conduction.
- [( )] B) Verapamil is a potent central alpha-2 agonist.
- [( )] C) Verapamil crosses the blood-brain barrier to inhibit the vagus nerve.
- [( )] D) Verapamil causes severe volume depletion, leading to a reflexive drop in heart rate.
***
**Rationale:** Verapamil is a non-dihydropyridine (non-DHP) CCB. Unlike DHP CCBs (like amlodipine) which only affect the vasculature, non-DHPs act directly on cardiac cells to slow conduction through the AV node and decrease heart rate (negative chronotropy).
* *Incorrect B:* It is not an alpha-2 agonist.
* *Incorrect C:* It does not inhibit the vagus nerve.
* *Incorrect D:* It does not cause severe volume depletion.
